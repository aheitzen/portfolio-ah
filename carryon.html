Carry On

Your next adventure awaits

Traveling can be one of the most rewarding experiences in life. However, travel can also be stressful, expensive and unpredictable. Carry On is a sister application to Alaska Airlines that offers experience and airfare bundles, making travel affordable and memorable. Carry On’s experiences offerings focus on outdoor adventure, historical executions, kid-friendly activities, locals only events, and food and cultural experiences.

Carry On partners with Alaska Air to offer discounted plane tickets if purchased with an add-on experience. Carry On supports Alaska Airlines by having users join their mileage plan program, encourages individuals to book with Alaska, rewards users for signing up with an Alaska Airlines credit card, and opens new doors of revenue and partnerships with Alaska Airlines. Carry On enhances travelers vacations and memories with unique and curated guest experiences.

The Opportunity

The primary opportunity for this project is to create a travel application that gives users discounted airfare and perks, while enhancing their vacations through unforgettable experiences. Carry On partners with Alaska Airlines to offer discounted plane tickets, when bundled with a Carry On adventure.

The secondary opportunity is to create a product from top-to-bottom. This project gives us the opportunity to conduct survey and in-person interviews, produce user experience deliverables, use those UX deliabels to build data-driven prototypes and interfaces, and finally create a tonally appropriate marketing campaign.

Project Goals

  • Collect user research using quantitative and qualitative methods
  • Synthesis user research into user experience assets
  • Build a prototype while referencing past research and user needs, motivations and pain points
  • Conduct user testing to validate prototype UI
  • Create a marketing campaign to promote application
  • Combine user research, user experience, user interface, and marketing in one comprehensive project

The Process

User experience research

Our first steps in research involved creating a series of surveys. We conducted online research to identify the best way to craft survey questions, with limited bias. We posted our surveys to the SCCA student Slack channel, social media accounts, emailed to friends and family, and to the subreddit r/SampleSize. Our surveys regularly received over 50 responses per survey. As we were collecting this data we also rewrote certain questions and introduced new questions as the answers begin to trend or stagnate in a certain direction.

Now that we had quantitative research, we wanted to conduct in-person interviews, and also find a population that was rooted in the key demographic we were seeking, in this case travelers. We conducted our first round of in-person interviews on the first floor of SCCA, and talked to students and professors. We then went to Sea-Tac light rail station and talked to travelers who were making their way back into the city from a recent trip. Both rounds of intervening yielded informative results, with more overlap and similarity than expected.

User experience design

Now that we had research data we needed to aggregate the data into something meaningful. We used a whiteboard and identified the most common needs, motivations and pain points from our participants and also closely analyzed participant quotes. Once we had a general sense of the data, we moved onto creating an affinity diagram to further consolidate and organize the results. From this research, we identified that our application should include five main features: help users select destinations based on their preferences and budget, recommend curated experiences at the destination, provide navigation in some capacity, include sharing and social media integration, and help users with common tips and tricks specific to their destination.

From there, we completed a SWOT analysis to better understand the travel and experience landscape. We analyzed three companies, Airbnb, Tripadvisor, and Expedia. Next, we took our understanding of our user base and created four personas that represented the participants form our research. To better align ourselves to our personas we also completed an empathy map exercise. Lastly, we completed four customer journey maps for our four personas to better understand their needs, wants, motiavions, pain points, and opportunities.

User interface design

Using research to inform our key features, simplified the prototyping process. Trevor and I divided the prototyping and used symbols in sketch to create unification. I designed the itinerary feature, that we synthesized from our personas, and Trevor designed the general booking process. We utilized complex reduction by using plenty of white-space and high-resolution aspirational images. After we had completed our lo-fi prototypes we implemented two rounds of user-testing to ensure our UI made sense.

It was during this time we also cemented our final logo and color pallete. We experimented with illustrative marks with nods to traveling, but utlemay found them too kitschy. We instead settled on Sara Script for the primary logo, with Open Sans as the secondary type choice. We utilized Alaska Airlines recently rebranded color pallet and exchanged the light green for a vibrant pink. We wanted the color pallet to feel related to Alaska Airlines but differentiated.

Marketing

The last step in the process was to identify and create marketing material for the app. I designed promotional cards, created a video, and made social media ads for Twitter and Facebook. The marketing and advertising space proved to be more creative and allowed focus on word play and sourcing beautiful images from around the globe.

User Research Execution

Constructing online surveys and conducting in-person interviews gave us valuable insight into our fledgling travel app idea. One of our main goals for this project was to make as few assumptions as possible, so we could create assets, applications, and experiences that were firmly user-centric and based in research. Below you will find summaries of our quantitative and qualitative research, quotes from our participants, and our key takeaways to include in the next steps of the project.

Quantitative survey results

After this project, I am convinced writing survey questions is an art form. Trevor and I sent out four surveys total. The first survey tested whether our experience application idea was interesting to other people. After that assumption was validated, we wanted to know the demographic makeup of our users, as well as drill down into their travel habits, travel motivations, and travel pain points.

Information from the survey was surprising and informative. Users identified that when traveling food, culture and points of interest were the highest rated activities as well as money and time being the biggest challenges associated with traveling. I was also surprised to learn that nearly half of our sample size, choose destinations by word-of-mouth. The key takeaways from our surveys identified our initial pain points and travel experiences that were rated most valuable by our users.

SCCA | In-person interviews

Now that we had our quantitative data, we wrote our first interview questions. We set up a table on the first floor of Seattle Central College, and lured students in with the promise of free cookies. We talked to over fifty students, and sought to understand their pain points while traveling, their great experiences while traveling, and how experiences at their vacation destination shaped their feelings around traveling.

From these interviews we were able to establish our five categories of experience offerings for our app: food and drink, outdoor adventure, historical excursions, locals only, and kid-friendly experiences. Affordability and connecting with friends and family was listed as the primary motivations for travel, and surprisingly, our participants generally did not use budgeting apps.

Noteworthy positive and negative experiences while traveling emerged throughout our interviews. Our participants valued comfort, safety, airline perks, and creating memories and experiences as being the most important while traveling, and transit delays, late flights, and technical mobile phone problems as being the biggest pain points.The main takeaways from our SCCA intrews can be found below.

Sea-Tac airport | In-person interviews

Our next round of interviews involved speaking directly to our target population. We made our way to the Sea-Tac light rail station to talk to travelers who had just returned to Seattle from a trip. We had a great response, with about 40 people being interviewed.

We adjusted questions from our earlier interviews, and also added new questions to our script. We wanted to know how long people planned for trips and what was it like traveling with friends and family. Our participants planned their trips at varying times, but overall it seemed to fall into four categories. Travelers planned for less than three months, three to six months, six to nine months, and more than a year. No participants interviewed planned over two years in advance.

In terms of traveling with friends and family, many of our participants reported having no conflict while traveling. However, some were more forthright and brought up scheduling conflicts, navigation struggles and general miscommunication as pain points.

Overall, many of the pain points our participants expressed were rooted in poor or miscommunication. Below is a summary of our interview findings as well as quotes from our participants.

Frugal Freddie

Freddie is an eighteen-year-old high school senior who is graduating in the next three months. She and a group of high school friends are planning their first international trip abroad to celebrate graduation before embarking for college. The destination is Costa Rica, and she has been working extra shifts at Fred Meyer’s to afford the international voyage. Because she is young and employed part-time, money is a serious concern for her. She also needs a secure and infallible way to reach her parents back home if there is an emergency and also wants to find experiences that the entire group can enjoy.

Final persona iteration | Frugal Freddie

Social Seth

Seth is a single childless thirty-five-year-old who works as a technical engineer. He travels for his job and has little time to reconnect with friends and family. He uses his vacation days when he can, because they do not roll-over into the next year. His primary needs include connecting with friends efficiently, finding unique and off the beaten path experiences, and maximizing his time to the fullest.

Final persona iteration | Social Seth

Empty Nester Eileen

Eileen is a fifty-year-old married mother of two who works as a human resources manager in Seattle. Both of her children have left for their first year of university, which has given Eileen space to create new goals and activities for herself and her husband. She has planned a trip with her husband to Cancun, and is looking for safe activities to do once there. Her core needs include redeeming existing Alaska Airlines Mileage points, finding safe and reliable activities, international safety tips, and an itinerary builder.

Final persona iteration | Empty Nester Eileen

Retired Richard

Richard is a sixty-eight-year-old retiree who is enjoying his retirement and prioritizes time with family and friends. He is looking forward to an annual family gathering in Maui, as he can use his Alaska Airlines Mileage points from his credit card. He is looking for a budgeting system that includes his points, as he is naturally good with money. His core needs include a budgeting system that integrates with his Alaska Airlines account, a list of items that are commonly forgotten, and experiences that are kid-friendly or historic in nature.

Final persona iteration | Retired Richard

Empathy Maps

Once our personas were established, we completed an empathy map exercise. This exercise aligned us to our personas to understand their fears and needs so we can better build products that enhance their vacations. For example, Freddie’s main goal was to successfully contact family back home. The main takeaways were Freddie feels fear around losing cell service and not having a backup in case of emergencies. This motivates us to explore offline communication and what current and emerging technologies can assist our users, regardless of where they are in the world.

Prototype

Now that we understand how Carry On and Alaska Airlines integrate, it was time to start working on the itenary prototype. The prototype features three primary sections: search and filter, experience details and booking, and billing and updated itinerary. The main goal for the user is to add a new experience to her vacation as time has opened up in her vacation itinerary.

View the live prototype link here.
Search and filter

Our user will identify that she has more time in her day than expected and wants to add a new item card to her Cancun vacation itinerary. She will tap “add a new experience” and will be taken to an input where she can search. Once tapped, she can either search by keywords or by category. Once she submits her search query, she will choose from curated experiences around her search parameters.

Experience details and booking

Now that our user has clicked on the experience detail page she can browse details related to that experience. This page will tell her the price point of the activity, the title and other logistic information like address and phone number, the experience summary, experience details, member summary, as well as access to reviews about this experience. Member summary includes how many Alaska Airlines points will be rewarding by booking this experience, as well as how close our members are to unlocking free flights.

Billing and checkout | Updated itinerary

Now that our user has decided she wants to purchase this experience and add it to her itinerary, she will be taken to a calendar UI. She will be presented with the available dates and choose the one that works best for her. She will select her preferred time and be directed to the purchasing page. Her method of payment has already been noted, and she reviews her purchase under the experience details header. After her purchase she is redirected to a confirmation screen where she can either return to the home page or add this item to her itinerary.

Marketing Execution

The final step in our Carry On journey involved creating marketing collateral to inform our audience of our new application. We provided proof of concept for two social media ads, promotional cards (than can double as postcard mailers), and a promotional video featuring one of Alaska’s most popular destinations, Maui.

This step in the process felt more creative as we were able to lean into information we had gathered from our competitor analysis. From an early date, we wanted to use vibrant, aspirational, and large-scale imagery to convey adventure for Carry On’s experiences. In marketing, we focus on using destinations as the main selling point, continue the thread with Carry On colors, and use simple rectangular shapes to provide movement and contrast where necessary.

Facebook advertising

For the Facebook ad, I focused on an outdoor excursion experience, and stayed away from island and tropical imagery. I used eye-catching imagery with unique colors to catch users attention while scrolling through their feed. The ad copy for this piece directly relates to the imagery and invokes a sense of adventure. I also placed the Carry On logo in the bottom left quadrant of the ad, as the skier is looking down at that angle.
